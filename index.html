Comparing 1940 and 1966

27 Sep 1940 (autumn) and 3 Jul 1966 (summer)

A4 motorway

The 1940 photo shows the area north of the District and Piccadilly railway lines as entirely residential. The 1966 photo shows the new motorway constructed in YEAR. Previously, the beautiful terraced houses north of the railway, constructed in YEAR for “bachelor artists”, looked out over the playing fields of PARK, which in YEAR gave way to the Hammersmith College (see 1966-81). Today those houses look out over 6 lanes of never-ending traffic.

The motorway was bulldozed through Victorian terraces purchased by compulsory purchase order [?], with many streets disconnected into a southern and a northern part. Previously traffic flowed along Hammersmith Road and King Street, badly unsuited to the volume of traffic.

Charing Cross Hospital

The 1940 photo shows the old Fulham Hospital, previously the Fulham Workhouse.

The 1966 shows a vast construction site, for the construction of the new Charing Cross Hospital, moved here from Charing Cross. This was given the go-ahead in DATE and was completed in DATE at the cost of £££, then the most expensive hospital project in the UK to date. See 1966-91 for its completion.

Field Road Estate

The 1940 photo shows the Victorian houses that made way for the Field Road housing estate, constructed in DATE, between Margravine Cemetery and Greyhound Road

The 1966 photo shows the estate nearing completion but not yet occupied. The tower block NAME, along Greyhound Road, appears to have been constructed at a later date.

Clem Attlee Estate and Normand Park

In 1940 the areas now occupied by Clem Attlee Estate and Normand Park were mostly uniform terraced housing. By 1966 this had been replaced south of Lillie Road by the distinctive 3-pointed tower blocks of Clem Attlee estate (built in 1952), and north of Lillie Road, by Normand Park with its bowling green. The area that became Fulham Pools in 1982 is still empty. St Augustine’s and Normand Croft schools are also new.

If you look at the map of 1945 bomb damage Mouse over to view, you will see that both these areas south and north of Lillie Road were extensively damaged.

From London Parks and Gardens Trust: Normand Park was created on the site of a C17th villa, Normand House, after WWII bombing severely damaged property in the area. In the C19th the house was used as an asylum, school and then a convent prior to its demolition. The LCC enclosed the bombed area for housing development and the public park, which opened in 1952. It was later extended to the south as further demolition took place and in 1971 it was transferred to Hammersmith & Fulham Council and completed with distinct areas providing playground, bowling green and sports areas. Fulham Pools opened here in 1982. In 2008 Normand Park re-opened after a major refurbishment project that has brought numerous new facilities.
